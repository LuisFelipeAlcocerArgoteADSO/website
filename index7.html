Crime and Punishment

The story narrates the life of Rodion Raskolnikov, a student in the capital of Imperial Russia, St. Petersburg, who is forced
to suspend his studies because of the misery in which he finds himself, despite the efforts of his mother Pulcheria and his
sister Dunia to send him money.

His sister, with the intention of helping him, accepts the marriage proposal of a rich man, which makes his brother angry when
he learns that she has accepted the proposal without having consulted him. But, although he does not want to accept the help,
Rodion has airs of grandeur, and in his delusions he believes he is worthy of a great future.

Raskolnikov decides to murder the old woman, not only with the aim of robbing her, in fact, he abandons her in a very lonely place
and does not make use of the loot that he quickly takes out of the moneylender's house after her murder, but also because he considers
her a human being useless to society. However, Raskolnikov's position is much more complex:

The crime leaves Rodion in a state of great confusion. For days, he is plunged into delirious fevers, caused by the act itself, wandering
aimlessly and losing track of reality at times, while at others he is terribly lucid. We are shown a mind as brilliant as it is disturbed,
obsessed by the act committed.

In his wanderings through St. Petersburg, Raskolnikov meets the former civil servant Marmeladov, a drinker who ends up dying, drunk, run
over by a horse; and his unfortunate family, whom Raskolnikov helps financially after Marmeladov's death, with the meager rubles he receives
from his mother. The eldest daughter of this family is Sonia, a very rebellious young girl who, in order to help her mother, prostitutes herself
to help her stepmother and her brothers, will be the only person to whom Raskolnikov explicitly confesses his crime.
